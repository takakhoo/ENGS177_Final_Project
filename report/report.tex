\begin{abstract}\noindent
We model dynamic stock-bond allocation as a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) whose latent state is the prevailing macro-financial regime. A two-state Gaussian Hidden Markov Model (HMM) is fit by Baum--Welch on the VIX and the 10Y--3M Treasury term spread; a recursive Bayesian filter maintains a belief $\bel_t$ over the latent regime, updated each month from the new observation. The associated fully-observable Markov Decision Process (MDP) is solved by both Value Iteration and exact Policy Iteration (cross-checked to agree within $2.6\times 10^{-6}$). The QMDP approximation \cite{littman1995}, which we derive explicitly from the belief-space Bellman equation as a one-step value-function approximation, selects the weight that maximises the belief-weighted action value $\sum_s \bel_t(s)\,Q^\ast(s,\mathbf a)$ at each rebalance. On a 2003--2026 backtest of SPY and AGG with monthly rebalancing and 5\,bps transaction costs, we run an eleven-baseline horse race spanning the academic and practitioner consensus (static 60/40, equal-weight, inverse-vol, risk-parity ERC, Markowitz with Ledoit--Wolf shrinkage, Faber 10-month SMA, time-series momentum, vol-targeting, HMM-conditional mean-variance, Black--Litterman with HMM-derived views, and myopic 12-month). At the canonical CRRA $\gamma{=}2$, the underlying MDP is genuinely regime-differentiated: the optimal policy is 100\% equity in the bull regime and 100\% bonds in the bear regime, and the resulting QMDP policy attains a Sharpe ratio of \textbf{1.18} (4th of twelve), comfortably beating the static 60/40 (0.81) and essentially tying the HMM-conditional mean-variance baseline (1.20), while cutting maximum drawdown from $-34\%$ to $-14\%$. The belief filter is genuinely recursive: $P(\text{bear}\mid\mathbf o_{1:t})$ tracks every NBER recession and the 2015--16 and 2022 stress episodes, and a single VIX spike flips the belief from $0.3\%$ to $98.7\%$ bear in one filter step. The honest verdict is that a correctly-specified POMDP regime overlay is a competitive risk-adjusted allocator that beats the static benchmark, though it does not overtake the practitioner-consensus trend rule (Faber, 1.68). Regime differentiation is robust across CRRA levels, observation cohorts, and three economically distinct sub-periods; expanding-window walk-forward refit \cite{nystrup2018} further improves it. We position QMDP within Powell's~\cite{powell2019} taxonomy as a value-function approximation with one-step direct lookahead.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%================================================

A long-only investor must choose, every month, the weights of a portfolio of two assets: a U.S.\ equity index (SPY) and a U.S.\ aggregate-bond index (AGG). The textbook 60/40 split is a \emph{static} allocation that implicitly assumes the joint return distribution is stable. Markets violate this in two specific ways: returns are conditionally heteroskedastic (calm decades broken by short crises), and stress periods exhibit higher cross-asset correlations so bond diversification erodes when it is most needed. In 2022 a 60/40 portfolio lost roughly 17\% as both legs sold off under persistent inflation, motivating tactical regime-aware overlays.

\paragraph{Research question.} We test the implicit claim of every regime-switching paper: \emph{Can a fully decision-theoretic regime overlay (formalised as a POMDP, fit to public macro-financial data, solved by a tractable belief-state heuristic) beat the static $60/40$ benchmark out-of-sample after realistic transaction costs?} By ``decision-theoretic'' we mean the allocation is the solution of an explicit optimisation, the latent regime is treated as a hidden state inferred by a Bayesian filter, and the policy is the QMDP approximation of an underlying POMDP. Three observations together force the POMDP formulation: the regime is latent, the observations are noisy, and decisions are sequential. Decision trees explode with horizon; influence diagrams blow up the same way for repeated rebalances; fully-observable MDPs assume what we cannot observe.

\paragraph{Contributions.} (i)~an explicit derivation of the QMDP rule from the belief-space Bellman equation, together with a fully-specified recursive belief filter and a worked numerical example (\Cref{sec:pomdp,sec:vi-pi}), the methodological core; (ii)~an eleven-baseline horse race spanning the academic and practitioner consensus (\Cref{sec:horserace}), in which the QMDP overlay beats the static 60/40 and ties the HMM-conditional mean-variance baseline; (iii)~robustness evidence that the regime-differentiated policy holds across CRRA levels (\Cref{sec:gamma-sweep}), observation cohorts (\Cref{sec:multifeature}), and three economically distinct sub-periods (\Cref{sec:subperiod}), with expanding-window walk-forward refit \cite{nystrup2018} a further improvement (\Cref{sec:walk-forward}); (iv)~a Powell-taxonomy positioning of QMDP as a VFA + one-step DLA; (v)~a reproducible public repository \texttt{takakhoo/ENGS177\_Final\_Project} with 14 data series, 12 strategies, and 13 experiment scripts.

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}
%================================================

\paragraph{Regime switching in finance.} The Markov-switching model is due to Hamilton \cite{hamilton1989} who applied a two-state model to U.S.\ GDP. Ang and Bekaert \cite{ang2002} demonstrated that ignoring regimes produces welfare losses in international allocation, but their gain mechanism is concentrated in the cash-switching margin: ``costs of ignoring regimes are small for all-equity portfolios.'' Guidolin and Timmermann \cite{guidolin2007} formalised multi-asset allocation under a 4-state HMM and reported certainty-equivalent gains of 1.5--3.0\% per annum. Nystrup et al.\ \cite{nystrup2018} address the look-ahead-bias problem via online walk-forward refit. Tu \cite{tu2010} examines whether regime switching matters once model, parameter, and regime uncertainty are jointly accounted for; the answer is qualified. A central methodological point of our study is that whether the regime label moves the optimal policy depends critically on the regime-conditional reward being estimated in the same space the HMM is calibrated in: with a correctly-applied belief filter, the label moves the policy even at the canonical low risk aversion ($\gamma{=}2$), a two-risky-asset universe notwithstanding.

\paragraph{POMDP framework.} A Partially Observable MDP \cite{kaelbling1998} assumes the agent observes only a noisy function of the latent state and maintains a probability distribution (belief) over the state. Exact POMDP solution is PSPACE-hard; the QMDP approximation \cite{littman1995} projects the belief onto the action-value function of the fully-observable MDP. We use QMDP because (i)~it is exact when the belief is a Dirac measure, (ii)~it reduces to a standard infinite-horizon MDP solver, and (iii)~in our setting there is no action that improves observability of the regime (we observe VIX and the term spread regardless of allocation), so the well-known weakness of QMDP (failure to motivate information-gathering actions \cite{hauskrecht2000}) does not bind. Point-based value iteration \cite{pineau2003} is a tighter alternative but unnecessary on our 2-state model with a 1-dimensional belief simplex.

\paragraph{Powell taxonomy positioning.} Powell \cite{powell2019} unifies sequential decision-making into four meta-classes: policy function approximations (PFAs), cost function approximations (CFAs), value function approximations (VFAs), and direct lookahead approximations (DLAs). QMDP fits naturally as a \emph{VFA} in which the belief-state value function $V(\bel)$ is approximated by the linear lower envelope $V_{\mathrm{QMDP}}(\bel) = \max_a \sum_s b(s) Q^\ast_{\mathrm{MDP}}(s,a)$, combined with a \emph{one-step DLA} at decision time. This sentence is the project's theoretical anchor: we are using the MDP value function as a VFA on the belief MDP and selecting actions via a one-step lookahead.

\paragraph{Practitioner consensus.} The empirical winner of published two-asset tactical horse races (Faber \cite{faber2007}, Hurst--Ooi--Pedersen ``Century of Evidence'' \cite{hurst2017}, Moskowitz--Ooi--Pedersen \cite{moskowitz2012}, Moreira--Muir \cite{moreira2017}) is consistently \emph{time-series momentum with volatility targeting and cash fallback}, reporting Sharpe 0.85--1.10 on SPY+AGG 1990--2024. This is the empirical bar our QMDP must clear.

\subsection{POMDP formulation}\label{sec:pomdp}

\begin{definition}[POMDP]\label{def:pomdp}
A POMDP is the tuple $(\mathcal{T},\state,\action,T,\Obs,O,R,\lambda)$ where $\mathcal{T}=\{0,1,\dots\}$ is the set of monthly epochs; $\state=\{s_1,\dots,s_K\}$ is the finite set of latent regimes; $\action=\{\mathbf{a}_1,\dots,\mathbf{a}_M\}$ is the discrete grid of long-only stock-bond weights; $T(s'\mid s)$ is the HMM transition matrix (action-independent because regimes are exogenous to portfolio choice); $\Obs=\R^d$; $O(\mathbf{o}\mid s)=\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{o};\boldsymbol{\mu}_s,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_s)$ is the Gaussian emission; the single-period reward is the expected CRRA utility of next month's \emph{gross} portfolio return,
\[
  R(s,\mathbf{a})=\E_{\mathbf{r}\mid s}\!\bigl[U_\gamma\!\bigl(1+\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{r}\bigr)\bigr],\qquad
  U_\gamma(w)=\tfrac{w^{1-\gamma}-1}{1-\gamma},
\]
with $\gamma{=}2$; and $\lambda=0.95$. Transaction costs ($c{=}5$\,bps of $\lVert\mathbf{a}_t-\mathbf{a}_{t-1}\rVert_1$) are \emph{not} embedded in $R$; they are charged in the backtest wealth accounting (\Cref{sec:protocol}), because the previous weight $\mathbf{a}_{t-1}$ is not part of the regime state, and folding it in would require augmenting the state space, which we deliberately do not do.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Recursive belief filter.} The agent never observes the regime; it maintains a belief $\bel_t\in\Delta^{K-1}$ and updates it each month from the new observation $\mathbf{o}_t$ by one step of the standard forward (Bayes) filter, in two stages:
\begin{align}
\text{(predict)}\quad & \hat\bel_t(s') = \textstyle\sum_s T(s'\mid s)\,\bel_{t-1}(s), \label{eq:predict}\\
\text{(update)}\quad  & \bel_t(s') = \frac{O(\mathbf{o}_t\mid s')\,\hat\bel_t(s')}{\sum_{s''}O(\mathbf{o}_t\mid s'')\,\hat\bel_t(s'')}. \label{eq:bayes}
\end{align}
The predict step advances last month's belief through the regime dynamics (it nudges the belief toward the other regime by the small switch probability); the update step multiplies by the Gaussian observation likelihood $O(\mathbf{o}_t\mid s')=\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{o}_t;\boldsymbol\mu_{s'},\boldsymbol\Sigma_{s'})$ and renormalises. We initialise $\bel_0$ at the stationary distribution of $T$ and run \cref{eq:predict,eq:bayes} forward over all 271 months; the resulting $P(\text{bear}\mid\mathbf{o}_{1:t})$ is plotted in \Cref{fig:belief}.

\paragraph{Worked example (a single filter step).} In August~2015, at the onset of the China/oil shock, the filter held $\bel_{t-1}=(\text{bull }0.997,\ \text{bear }0.003)$. The predict step \eqref{eq:predict} moves this to $\hat\bel_t=(0.985,\,0.015)$. That month's observation (VIX$=28.4$) is $\approx\!5{,}000\times$ more likely under the bear emission than the bull emission, so the update step \eqref{eq:bayes} flips the posterior to $\bel_t=(\text{bull }0.013,\ \text{bear }0.987)$ in one step. The belief is genuinely data-driven and responsive; it is not a label assigned by hand.

\subsection{HMM calibration via Baum--Welch}\label{sec:hmm}

The Gaussian HMM joint density factors as $p(\mathbf{o}_{1:T},s_{1:T})=\pi_{s_1}O(\mathbf{o}_1\mid s_1)\prod_{t=2}^T T(s_t\mid s_{t-1})O(\mathbf{o}_t\mid s_t)$. Baum--Welch is the EM algorithm tailored to this factorisation: the E-step computes forward $\alpha_t(s)=p(\mathbf{o}_{1:t},s_t{=}s)$ and backward $\beta_t(s)=p(\mathbf{o}_{t+1:T}\mid s_t{=}s)$ messages; the smoothed posterior is $\gamma_t(s)=\alpha_t(s)\beta_t(s)/\sum_s \alpha_t(s)\beta_t(s)$; the M-step updates parameters by weighted MLEs \cite{rabiner1989}. We use \texttt{hmmlearn.GaussianHMM} with \texttt{covariance\_type='full'}, five random restarts, tolerance $10^{-4}$, on observations standardised by the 2003--2014 training-window mean and standard deviation; all downstream \texttt{predict} and belief-filter calls standardise with these \emph{same} statistics, so the model is always evaluated in the space it was calibrated in. Model selection across $K\in\{2,3,4\}$ uses BIC: $K{=}2$ is preferred at 271 monthly observations on $d{=}2$ channels. The estimated transition matrix is sticky, $T\approx\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0.99 & 0.01\\ 0.02 & 0.98\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, with stationary distribution $(0.64,0.36)$. Over the full sample the MAP regime assignment gives a bull regime of 201 months (mean SPY $+1.28\%$/mo, mean VIX 15.8) and a bear regime of 71 months (mean SPY $-0.22\%$/mo, mean VIX 28.1), a genuinely defensive bear state, which is what makes the regime label move the optimal policy in \Cref{sec:vi-pi}.

\subsection{MDP solvers and QMDP}\label{sec:vi-pi}

The underlying MDP's optimal value function satisfies the Bellman equation $V^\ast(s)=\max_a\{R(s,a)+\lambda\sum_{s'}T(s'\mid s)V^\ast(s')\}$. Value iteration applies $V^{(n+1)}=LV^{(n)}$ and stops at $\|V^{(n+1)}-V^{(n)}\|_\infty<\varepsilon(1-\lambda)/(2\lambda)=2.6\times 10^{-6}$ for $\lambda{=}0.95,\varepsilon{=}10^{-4}$, converging in 133 iterations. Policy iteration solves the linear system $(\mathbf{I}-\lambda\mathbf{P}_\pi)V=\mathbf{r}_\pi$, then greedy-improves; converges in 2 iterations.

\begin{proposition}[Solver agreement]\label{prop:agreement}
On our calibrated MDP with $\lambda{=}0.95$, $\gamma{=}2$, VI and PI produce identical greedy policies and value functions agreeing within $2.6\times 10^{-6}$ component-wise, the empirical realisation of the contraction-mapping equivalence~\cite{puterman}.
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{From the belief-space Bellman equation to QMDP.} The POMDP is equivalent to a fully-observable MDP whose state is the belief $\bel$ (the \emph{belief MDP}). Its optimal value obeys
\begin{equation}\label{eq:belief-bellman}
V^\ast(\bel)=\max_{\mathbf{a}\in\action}\Bigl\{\underbrace{\textstyle\sum_s \bel(s)R(s,\mathbf{a})}_{R(\bel,\mathbf{a})}
+\lambda\sum_{\mathbf{o}}\Prb(\mathbf{o}\mid\bel,\mathbf{a})\,V^\ast\!\bigl(\bel'(\bel,\mathbf{a},\mathbf{o})\bigr)\Bigr\},
\end{equation}
where $\bel'$ is the posterior from \cref{eq:predict,eq:bayes}. Solving \cref{eq:belief-bellman} exactly is PSPACE-hard: the value function is piecewise-linear-convex over the continuous simplex with a number of facets that can grow doubly-exponentially in the horizon. The \textbf{QMDP approximation} \cite{littman1995,hauskrecht2000} replaces the intractable continuation term with the assumption that \emph{the state becomes fully observable immediately after the current action}. Under that single assumption the continuation value from any successor state $s'$ is just the underlying fully-observable MDP value $V^\ast(s')$, so the belief-action value collapses to a belief-average of the MDP $Q$-function:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:qmdp-derivation}
\textstyle
Q_{\mathrm{QMDP}}(\bel,\mathbf{a})
=\sum_s \bel(s)\bigl[R(s,\mathbf{a})+\lambda\sum_{s'}T(s'\mid s)V^\ast(s')\bigr]
=\sum_s \bel(s)\,Q^\ast(s,\mathbf{a}),
\end{equation}
with $Q^\ast(s,\mathbf{a})=R(s,\mathbf{a})+\lambda\sum_{s'}T(s'\mid s)V^\ast(s')$ the action-value of the MDP we solved above. The decision rule is then the one-step lookahead
\begin{equation}\label{eq:qmdp}
\boxed{\;\pi_{\mathrm{QMDP}}(\bel)=\argmax_{\mathbf{a}\in\action}\sum_{s\in\state}\bel(s)\,Q^\ast(s,\mathbf{a}).\;}
\end{equation}
Three properties make this a principled choice rather than a heuristic. \emph{(i) Exactness at certainty:} if $\bel=\mathbf{e}_s$ (a Dirac on regime $s$), \cref{eq:qmdp} reduces to the exact MDP policy $\argmax_a Q^\ast(s,a)$. \emph{(ii) Upper bound:} $V_{\mathrm{QMDP}}(\bel)=\max_a Q_{\mathrm{QMDP}}(\bel,\mathbf a)\ge V^\ast(\bel)$ pointwise, because giving the agent free state information after one step can only help \cite{hauskrecht2000}; QMDP is thus an optimistic but consistent approximation. \emph{(iii) The one assumption it makes is harmless here:} QMDP cannot value information-gathering actions (it assumes the state is about to be revealed for free), but in our problem \emph{no action affects observability} (we observe VIX and the term spread regardless of how we allocate), so $\Prb(\mathbf{o}\mid\bel,\mathbf{a})$ in \cref{eq:belief-bellman} is action-independent and the only thing QMDP gives up is exactly the thing that does not matter. Point-based value iteration \cite{pineau2003} would tighten the bound but is unnecessary on our one-dimensional belief simplex. In Powell's \cite{powell2019} taxonomy, \cref{eq:qmdp} is a \emph{value-function approximation} (the MDP $Q^\ast$ used as a linear functional of the belief) composed with a \emph{one-step direct lookahead}.

\paragraph{What we actually computed.} The action space is the $M{=}6$-point grid $\action=\{(w_S,1{-}w_S):w_S\in\{0,0.2,\dots,1\}\}$, so $Q^\ast$ is a $2\times 6$ table. The reward $R$ is built by Monte Carlo: for each regime we draw $5{,}000$ samples of the joint $(\text{SPY},\text{AGG})$ monthly return from that regime's empirical conditional distribution (regimes assigned by the HMM \emph{in standardised observation space}, matching calibration) and average $U_2(1+\mathbf a^\top\mathbf r)$. At $\gamma{=}2$ the resulting $Q^\ast$ is monotonically increasing in the stock weight for the bull regime (so $\pi^\ast(\text{bull})=100/0$) and monotonically decreasing for the bear regime (so $\pi^\ast(\text{bear})=0/100$): the regime label genuinely moves the policy. The belief from \cref{eq:predict,eq:bayes} then mixes these two columns, so the deployed weight tilts continuously toward bonds as $\bel_t(\text{bear})$ rises.

\subsection{Backtest protocol and metrics}\label{sec:protocol}

Walk-forward over 271 monthly observations 2003-10-31 to 2026-04-30. Monthly rebalancing. Transaction cost $5\times 10^{-4}\,\lVert w_t-w_{t-1}\rVert_1$ per period. Each policy initialised at the stationary regime distribution and $(0.6,0.4)$ weights. Performance metrics (in \texttt{src/utils/metrics.py}): CAGR, annualised volatility and downside vol, Sharpe and Sortino ratios, Omega ratio, max drawdown, Calmar, Ulcer index, tail ratio, hit rate, average $\ell_1$ turnover, and information ratio vs.\ benchmark.

We split the sample into three economically meaningful sub-periods and re-run the horse race (\Cref{fig:subperiod}). \section{Discussion and Conclusions}\label{sec:conclusions}
%================================================

The narrative that emerges from \Cref{sec:horserace,sec:gamma-sweep,sec:multifeature,sec:walk-forward,sec:subperiod} is coherent: a correctly-specified POMDP regime overlay is a genuinely competitive risk-adjusted allocator. At the canonical $\gamma{=}2$ the underlying MDP is regime-differentiated (100\% equity in the bull regime, 100\% bonds in the bear regime), the recursive belief filter mixes those two columns into a continuously regime-aware weight, and the resulting QMDP policy attains Sharpe~1.18: beating the static 60/40 (0.81) and the classical risk-based baselines, tying HMM-conditional mean-variance (1.20), and cutting maximum drawdown from $-34\%$ to $-14\%$. This regime differentiation is not an artifact of any single modelling choice: it holds across CRRA levels (\Cref{tab:gamma-policy}), across observation cohorts (\Cref{tab:cohort}), and across three economically distinct sub-periods (\Cref{fig:subperiod}), and expanding-window walk-forward refit (\Cref{tab:walk-forward}) more than halves the drawdown again.

Our findings align with the literature: Guidolin and Timmermann \cite{guidolin2007} predict that financial-stress channels sharpen the regime model (our +Stress cohort attains the lowest BIC); Nystrup et al.\ \cite{nystrup2018} predict that expanding-window refit improves a regime overlay (it does, chiefly on drawdown); and Ang and Bekaert \cite{ang2002} caution that gains in a two-risky-asset universe with no risk-free leg are bounded, consistent with our overlay being competitive but not dominant against trend-following. We position the contribution precisely within Powell's \cite{powell2019} four-class taxonomy: QMDP is a value-function approximation (the MDP $Q^\ast$ as a linear functional of the belief) composed with a one-step direct lookahead, derived here from the belief-space Bellman equation rather than asserted.

\paragraph{Three extensions.} \emph{(i)~CVaR-penalised reward.} Replace the CRRA reward with $R(s,\mathbf{a})=\E[\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{r}\mid s]-\kappa\,\mathrm{CVaR}_\alpha[\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{r}\mid s]$ to penalise bear-regime tail risk directly; a one-line change in \texttt{src/utils/utility.py} that we expect to narrow the gap to trend-following further. \emph{(ii)~Point-based VI \cite{pineau2003}.} Maintain explicit $\alpha$-vectors over a small reachable-belief set for a tight upper bound on QMDP; negligible cost on a 1-D simplex. \emph{(iii)~Off-policy MC + Double Q-learning \cite{precup2000,vanhasselt2010}.} A genuinely model-free cross-check that does not assume the QMDP transition matrix; agreement with our model-based $Q^\ast$ would corroborate the calibration.

\paragraph{Honest verdict.} A correctly-applied POMDP regime overlay (QMDP on a belief recursively filtered from VIX and the term spread) beats the static 60/40 on every risk-adjusted metric and across every sub-period we tested, while roughly tying the best model-based mean-variance alternative. It does \emph{not} overtake the practitioner-consensus trend rule (Faber's 10-month SMA, Sharpe~1.68) on our 2003--2026 sample, and we make no such claim. What we do claim is sharper than a Sharpe number: the POMDP is the right minimum decision-theoretic tool here; the belief state is genuinely recursive and demonstrably regime-tracking (\Cref{fig:belief}); the QMDP rule follows from the belief-space Bellman equation as an explicit one-step value-function approximation; and the regime-aware policy that results is robust across risk aversion, observations, sub-periods, and refit cadence.
